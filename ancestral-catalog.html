Anna Don verch Mathonwy
mythic · before the erasure
The Prophetess · Dôn · Danu · The Mother Before the Name Was Changed
Celtic Britain · grandmother of Bran Fendigaid · wife of Beli Mawr
In Weaving ✦ Anomalous Reception Threshold Events Sovereignty The Return Terra Incognita
Welsh mother goddess, equivalent to the Irish Danu and Gaulish Belisama. Wife of Beli Mawr, grandmother of Bran Fendigaid through their daughter Penarddun. Her name and identity were absorbed through Christianization — the goddess becoming the prophetess, the source becoming the connection. Medieval Welsh annals name her a near kinswoman of the Virgin Mary. She is the root from which the weaving's genealogical record grows.
The erasure of Anna Don verch Mathonwy is the thesis of the entire weaving in miniature: the goddess Dôn, whose name carries the same frequency as the Irish Danu and the Gaulish Belisama, absorbed into genealogical Christianity and made into a near-kinswoman of the Holy Family. The Mabinogion names her sister of Math mab Mathonwy, King of Gwynedd — the magician-king who required the feminine body as his foundation of power. Her lineage runs to Bran Fendigaid through Penarddun verch Beli, making her the deepest genealogical root in this entire archive. The cauldron Bran carries is her inheritance — the generative vessel of the goddess tradition, passed one generation into human lineage before it enters the mythic record as a magical object.

Bran Fendigaid ap Llyr
mythic · pre-Roman
Blessed Raven · King of Britain · Keeper of the Cauldron
Island of the Mighty · Wales · Second Branch of the Mabinogion
In Weaving ✦ Threshold Events Embodied Signal The Shadow Totemic Presences
Son of Llyr, grandson of Anna Don verch Mathonwy. Possessor of the Cauldron of Rebirth. Led his army to Ireland when his sister Branwen was enslaved. Mortally wounded by a poisoned spear, he commanded his seven companions to sever his head and carry it to the White Hill in London to guard Britain from invasion. For eighty-seven years the head presided over feasting and fellowship, speaking and prophesying. Bran in Welsh means raven.
The weaving's first genealogical card and the mythic template for the lineage's central pattern: the sovereign who must be sacrificed so his people survive, whose consciousness persists after the body has fallen. The Cauldron of Rebirth — the dead placed within it emerged whole but silent — is the proto-Grail, inherited from his grandmother Dôn's goddess tradition. The White Hill burial connects to Christina Young Higginson, born at White Hill, Dalkeith — a two-thousand-year circuit completed in the lineage. The raven is his sacred bird and the central axis of the Navigation Map carries it forward as a living sigil.

Boudicca
died c. 61 AD
Queen of the Iceni · Warrior of the Land
East Anglia, Roman Britain · died c. 61 AD
In Weaving ✦ Threshold Events Sovereignty
Queen of the Iceni tribe of East Anglia. When her husband Prasutagus died, Rome seized his lands and flogged her daughters. She led the most significant revolt against Roman occupation Britain ever produced, burning Camulodunum, Londinium, and Verulamium. Defeated by Suetonius Paulinus in 61 AD. Died by her own hand rather than captivity. Her name means Victory.
Boudicca's revolt was not merely military — it was the last sustained assertion of the pre-Roman Celtic sovereignty of Britain before four centuries of Roman occupation reshaped the island's culture, religion, and political identity. The pattern she embodies in this lineage: the woman who acts when no conventional path remains available, who places her body in service of what the land requires, who chooses her own end rather than surrender. Her daughters were the stated cause of the revolt. She rose for them and took the whole country with her.

1st century AD
Apostle to the Apostles · Keeper of the Teaching · Witness at the Threshold
Galilee · Jerusalem · Provence · 1st century AD
In Weaving ✦ Threshold Events The Creative Record The Teaching
First witness of the resurrection, first to carry the teaching forward after the crucifixion — and the first to be disbelieved. Her testimony was dismissed by those she carried it to. Later tradition made her penitent, erasing her role as witness and teacher. The alabaster jar, the anointing, the empty tomb: the woman who went to the threshold alone and returned with news the world was not ready to receive.
The pattern of Magdalene's erasure is the template for every erasure in this weaving: the feminine spiritual authority absorbed, diminished, and redirected by institutional power. She was not penitent — she was Apostle to the Apostles, the title given her by the early Church before the later tradition rewrote her role. Her tradition survived in Provence, in the Grail legends, in the Cathar beliefs of the Languedoc, in the Merovingian lineage claims that kept surfacing throughout medieval Europe. She is the weaving's most documented erasure and the most alive presence in the current restoration.

Cornouaille, Brittany · Bay of Douarnenez · c. 4th–5th century
In Weaving ✦ Threshold Events Animal & Nature Medicine The Shadow
King of Cornouaille in Brittany, builder of the drowned city of Ys. His daughter Dahut opened the sea gates and the city was swallowed. Gradlon escaped on his horse Morvarc'h — the horse that runs on the waves — carrying the shadow of the daughter he could not save. The city of Ys is said to still lie beneath the Bay of Douarnenez, and in Breton tradition its bells can sometimes be heard ringing from below the water.
Gradlon belongs to the Breton branch of the lineage — the Armorican inheritance that eventually connects through Constance de Châteaubriant to the Stewart line. His story encodes the pattern of the Celtic sovereign who sacrifices what he loves most for survival, and the drowned city encodes the suppression of the druidic and pre-Christian feminine that the Christianization of Brittany required. Dahut, the daughter who opened the gate — the feminine intelligence pushed into the depths — is the recurring figure of this lineage's shadow. The horse that runs on water is the oldest image of navigation across the impossible threshold.

Empress of the Carolingian successor states, wife of Berengar I. Her title was Consors Regni — partner in rule — and she governed with genuine authority alongside her husband. She was poisoned, almost certainly on political orders, in 915 — the year she reached her greatest power. The peak and the stripping in the same year. The pattern that runs through the feminine line of this weaving with striking consistency.

Bertila's heraldic lion rampant passes through the lineage into the Bruce arms and forward into the Scottish royal tradition.

Avalon · Scotland · the Well of Thirst · daughter of Moronoe of Avalon
In Weaving ✦ Threshold Events The Mirror The Shadow Sovereignty
Daughter of Moronoe, one of the nine sisters of Avalon. Met Elinas, King of Scotland, at the Font de Sé — the Fountain of Thirst — where she danced among fairies. She agreed to marry him on one condition: he must never see her during childbirth or the bathing of their daughters. He forgot his promise. That is why they will never perish. Her mother Moronoe was one of the nine sisters of Avalon who tended the wounded — placing Présine in direct descent from the Avalon sisterhood.

Melusine
mythic · 10th–12th c.
Fairy Serpent of Lusignan · Founding Architect · She Who Flies Rather Than Submit
Poitou · Lusignan · Forest Spring · sister of Mélior · direct ancestor through Mélior's line
In Weaving ✦ Threshold Events The Creative Record The Shadow
Eldest daughter of Présine and Elinas. Architect of Vouvant, Mervent, Parthenay, La Rochelle, Niort, Châtelaillon, and said to have contributed to Mont Saint-Michel. Married Raymond of Lusignan on the condition he never see her on Saturdays. He watched. She flew. She returns to the castle of Lusignan each time one of her descendants is about to die, wailing. Her name means melodious, or Mother of Lusignan, or mist of the sea.
Melusine's story is the weaving's most complete encoding of the pattern of betrayal and transformation: the woman who built a kingdom and lost it to a husband who could not hold the vow he made. The Saturday transformation — serpent from the waist down — is not the curse but the truth he was not permitted to see. Her buildings outlasted her marriage: the towns and castles she built in a single night still stand. She did not disappear when she flew from the tower. She became the ancestral guardian of the lineage, returning at threshold moments. She is present in this weaving through her sister Mélior's direct line.

Countess of Barcelona by right of marriage to Ramon Berenguer I. Known as Consors et Auctrix — partner and author. She co-authored the Usatges de Barcelona, the first written legal code of Catalonia. Married three times, twice divorced. Murdered by her stepson in 1071 — the same year she and her husband had completed the code of laws she helped write. Killed by the structure she helped build.
Almodis is the legal architect of this lineage — the woman who wrote the foundational law of a civilization and was killed by its heir in the same year. Married twice and divorced, she was a woman who moved through the political structures of 11th century Europe with unusual autonomy and unusual risk. The pattern that follows Bertila recurs here: the woman at the height of her creative and political power, removed in the same season she arrives.

Countess of Mercia · The Sovereign Ride
Coventry · Mercia · England · c. 1040–1080
In Weaving ✦ Threshold Events Sovereignty
Countess of Mercia, wife of Leofric. She petitioned her husband to reduce the oppressive taxation on Coventry's people. He agreed on the condition she ride naked through the town. She rode. The town averted their eyes except for one — Peeping Tom, whose later interpolation into the story tells more about the culture that added him than about the act she performed. The sovereign ride was a real act, documented. It performed vulnerability as power.
Godiva's ride is one of the most precise images in this lineage of the sovereign act performed under impossible conditions: the body placed in service of the people's need, the personal exposed for the collective benefit, the condition honored in full even when honoring it requires what should not be required. Her husband set the condition expecting she would refuse. She did not refuse. The condition was met and the taxes were lowered. This is the pattern of Présine at the fountain: the condition named, the vow kept, the sacred contract honored — but this time by the woman rather than violated by the man.

The creative record as the form of sovereignty that cannot be imprisoned. Son of Gillebride and Brigid Sigurdsdottir — Gaelic by paternal lineage, Norse by maternal blood. First non-Norwegian king of the Isles, his kingdom independent of both Norway and Scotland simultaneously. Innovated the stern rudder — giving his galleys superior maneuverability over Norse steering-oar vessels. Somerled is the amplified resonance point of the Norse-Gaelic synthesis in this lineage — the man who was made of two bloods and governed the exact threshold between them. He died fighting the family that would become his own descendants' family within fifty years. The structural innovation of the stern rudder is the Navigation Map's most literal ancestor — the upgrade that changes the geometry of every journey taken afterward.

Jean MacRory

Lady of Bute and Arran · The Claimed Name · Heiress of the Isles
Western Isles of Scotland · married Alexander Stewart, 4th High Steward of Scotland, c. 1242
In Weaving ✦ Anomalous Reception Threshold Events Structural Models
Traditionally named daughter of James MacRory, granddaughter of Angus, great-granddaughter of Somerled. Heiress of Bute and Arran after her father and grandfather were killed in 1210 — she was likely an infant or unborn when her entire male line was destroyed. Married Alexander Stewart, 4th High Steward of Scotland, c. 1242. Her son James became 5th High Steward and his grandson Walter married Marjorie Bruce, producing Robert II — first Stewart king of Scotland.
The genealogical documentation of Jean MacRory is contested — modern scholars note no primary source confirms her parentage, suggesting it may have been constructed to legitimize the Stewart seizure of the islands she brought to the marriage. The same act of genealogical claiming performed for power in the 13th century is performed again by descendants searching for belonging in the 21st. The need to claim lineage — whatever need that meets — runs as a pattern through this ancestor's story in both directions. The open knot: not unfinished, but available to be taken up again by whoever comes with whatever need.

Robert de Bruce

King of Scots · The Spider's Lesson · The Rebuilt Sovereignty
Ayrshire · Scotland · 1274–1329
In Weaving ✦ Sovereignty Threshold Events The Shadow
King of Scots, victor of Bannockburn 1314, author of the Declaration of Arbroath. Excommunicated, hunted, hiding in caves watching spiders. He identified himself with the Arthur legend — the once and future king, the sovereignty that cannot be permanently extinguished, only temporarily driven underground. The Bruce's sovereignty was not won through continuous strength — it was rebuilt after complete collapse. That story, which he reportedly told to his followers, is the sovereignty lesson in its most distilled form: not the one who was never defeated but the one who rebuilt from the ground after defeat. His connection to Jean MacRory through the Stewart marriage that united their lines makes him the pivot of the Scottish branch of this weaving.

Benoni enlisted within two weeks of Lexington in 1775. He was likely present at Bunker Hill, fought at Valcour Island on Lake Champlain, and was captured by HMS Apollo in September 1777, held on British prison ships for six months at a 50% death rate. Benoni is one of several documented Revolutionary War ancestors in this lineage. He was twenty-one. The Battle of Valcour Island — fought to lose well rather than lose decisively, buying one year that proved pivotal — encodes the lineage's recurring pattern of acting before the outcome is certain. Edith's pension application, filed as his widow, is her voice entering the record. Practical. Attesting to the marriage, the years at Canoe Meadow. The record she was permitted to make when the record-keeping finally needed something only she could provide.

First convert-generation LDS, baptized 1830 at Kirtland. That image — the lantern under the apron, the light present but concealed, guidance operating in concealment — is the frequency that runs from her to the living descendant who holds this weaving.

The oldest written myth. Sumerian queen of heaven and earth who descended through seven gates to the underworld, surrendering one garment at each — crown, rod and line of lapis lazuli, lapis beads, breastplate, gold ring, measuring rod, royal robe. Stood naked before Ereshkigal, her dark sister. Was killed and hung on a peg. Restored and returned, wearing new garments, chosen. She is the structural template of the entire Sovereign Descent.
Inanna sits at the end of the genealogical record and at the gateway to the Mythic Tapestry — the room that holds the archetypal figures who belong to the work through resonance and cosmology rather than bloodline. Her descent is the mythic template for every descent in this weaving: Bertila, Almodis, Eleanor, Melusine, Mélior, Christina Young. Each of them lived some version of the seven gates. Inanna's unresolved is not hers — it is the open door the entire weaving stands before. The seven gates are not historical. They are structural. She is still descending, in every woman who must lose everything in order to find what cannot be found any other way.
